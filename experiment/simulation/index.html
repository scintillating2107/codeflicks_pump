Theory

Centrifugal Pump Fundamentals

A centrifugal pump is a dynamic pump that uses rotating impellers to increase the pressure and flow rate of a fluid. The pump consists of several key components working together to transfer mechanical energy to fluid energy.

Key Components:

  • Impeller: The rotating component that imparts kinetic energy to the fluid
  • Volute Casing: Converts kinetic energy to pressure energy
  • Suction Eye: Inlet where fluid enters the pump
  • Discharge: Outlet where pressurized fluid exits
  • Drive Shaft: Transfers power from motor to impeller

Working Principle:

The centrifugal pump operates based on the principle of centrifugal force. When the impeller rotates, it creates a low-pressure zone at the center (eye), causing fluid to be drawn in. The rotating impeller then accelerates the fluid radially outward, increasing its kinetic energy. The volute casing converts this kinetic energy into pressure energy, forcing the fluid out through the discharge.

Energy Transfer Process:

The energy transfer in a centrifugal pump involves three stages:

  • Electrical to Mechanical: Motor converts electrical energy to rotational mechanical energy
  • Mechanical to Kinetic: Impeller transfers mechanical energy to fluid as kinetic energy
  • Kinetic to Pressure: Volute converts kinetic energy to pressure energy
